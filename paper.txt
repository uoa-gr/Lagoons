Abstract. Coastal lagoons are unique ecosystems that can provide a wide range of environmental services, as well as benefits to humanity. However, they are prone to sea-level rise (SLR), due to both human activities and climate change. The Greek coastline is characterized by several coastal lagoons. In this paper, we digitized all coastal lagoons along the Greek coastline (a total of 152 lagoons), using satellite images. We then calculated their dimensions and physiographical characteristics. Furthermore, we conducted a multi-temporal analysis using Sentinel-2 imagery (2015–2025) to assess water surface extent and water quality trends for the five largest lagoons. Additionally, we made an attempt to highlight the magnitude of the effects of the future sea-level rise to them, by considering two Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5) scenarios for future sea-level rise in the Mediterranean context. Where there were available projections also considering Vertical Land Motion (VLM), we used those instead. Our results show that 8 to 25% of the Greek lagoons will be fully inundated, depending on the scenario considered, but much more of them will be partly breached. Regarding the largest lagoons, remote sensing analysis revealed predominantly stable conditions, with the exception of Tsoukali lagoon, which exhibited a significant expansion trend; likely linked to sediment starvation caused by upstream dam construction. In terms of water quality, while all five major lagoons remain eutrophic, Vistonis was identified as the most ecologically stressed system with recorded anoxia events, whereas the shallow bathymetry of the Amvrakikos and Kleisova lagoons appears to provide inherent resilience against stratification-induced anoxia. Collectively, these findings underscore the critical susceptibility of Greek coastal lagoons to both climatic and anthropogenic stressors. Consequently, future management strategies must prioritize not only adaptation to sea-level rise but also the mitigation of local pressures, specifically sediment deficit and nutrient loading, to ensure the long-term resilience of these ecosystems.
Keywords: coastal lagoons; Greek coastline; inundation; sea-level changes
Introduction
Coastal lagoons are transitional waterbodies between continental and marine systems (Pérez-Ruzafa et al. 2019). Typically, they are partly of wholly segregated from the open sea through a coastal barrier, which is in most cases parallel to the coastline (Björn Kjerfve and Magill 1989; B. Kjerfve 1994). In many cases, they may come into direct connection with the sea instead of being completely secluded (Esteves et al. 2008). This allows for water exchange between the sea and the lagoons, which enables salinity regulation and nutrient and sediment transport. Lagoons are created by the accumulation of sediment and the formation of barriers by waves and longshore currents. Sedimentation, barrier formation and vertical tectonic movements may also influence lagoon development (El Mahrad et al. 2022).
Coastal lagoons are dynamic and complex ecosystems that provide a wide range of services (Derolez et al. 2023). Coastal lagoons have great ecological and economic significance and are among the most productive ecosystems (Pérez-Ruzafa et al. 2024). According to several authors (e.g. (Canu et al. 2012; Carrasco et al. 2016; El Mahrad et al. 2022), coastal lagoons are significant ecosystems that offer a variety of ecological functions, such as nutrient cycling, water purification and habitats for different species. Because of their shallow, nutrient-rich waters and the presence of a variety of habitats, e.g. salt marshes, seagrass beds, and mangroves, they are frequently characterized by high biodiversity and productivity (Canu et al. 2012; Carrasco et al. 2016; Pérez-Ruzafa et al. 2024). Various human activities such as fishing, aquaculture and tourism rely on the services provided by coastal lagoons (Chapman 2012; Richardson and Nicholls 2021).
A very significant service provided by coastal lagoons is greenhouse gas sequestration. For example, the lagoonal water can capture CO2 from the atmosphere, and so does the wetland vegetation (if present) (Mcleod et al. 2011; Mao et al. 2021). In this way, coastal lagoons help mitigate the global effects of climate change, by reducing the greenhouse gases. In fact, it has been estimated that coastal wetlands contribute to the removal of up to 10 times more CO2 per unit area that other environments, such as tropical rainforests (Mcleod et al. 2011).
Coastal marshes are also very effective in mitigating coastal erosion (Wang et al. 2017). Coastal lagoons have been shown to mitigate the incoming wave energy (Möller et al. 2014; Fairchild et al. 2021). They are effective not only against typical waves, but against storm surges as well (Van Coppenolle et al. 2018). The vegetation which is often present can also be very effective in mitigating the wave energy (Möller et al. 2014). Similarly, coastal lagoons are effective in reducing the effects of coastal inundation (Fairchild et al. 2021; Inácio et al. 2023). They have therefore proposed by many researchers as potential soft engineering measures for coastal protection (Narayan et al. 2017; Fairchild et al. 2021).
Coastal lagoons are susceptible to numerous hazards, arising from both natural (like storm surges (Niki Evelpidou et al. 2022)) and human sources (such as overfishing, pollution and land use changes (drainage/reclamation for agriculture, urbanization, or constructions for tourism purposes). For example, storm surges along with extreme weather can cause erosion, sedimentation and/or changes in the quality of the water in coastal lagoons (Chapman 2012). In addition to natural processes, they are also endangered by a range of human activities (Gatto and Carbognin 1981; Chapman 2012; Carrasco et al. 2016).
Sea-level rise (SLR), however, remains one of the greatest hazards for coastal lagoons, as it can cause flooding, erosion of the lagoon margins and an increase in the salinity of the lagoon’s waters (Carrasco et al. 2016). Coastal lagoons are particularly vulnerable to sea-level rise, due to their shallow depth (Niki Evelpidou, Petropoulos, et al. 2021a; Niki Evelpidou et al. 2022), low-lying location, and dependence on barrier stability (Conde et al. 2019) and sediment input (Hzami et al. 2024). Rising seas can lead to lagoon inundation, increased salinity intrusion, shoreline retreat, and, in extreme cases, barrier breaching or lagoon drowning. The system's resilience largely depends on whether sediment accretion rates can match or exceed sea level rise (Zanchettin et al. 2007; Conde et al. 2019). If sediment supply is insufficient or barrier stability is compromised, the lagoon may transition into an open embayment.
Coastal barriers are in most cases unconsolidated and, thus, highly dynamic landforms, often rapidly responding to environmental changes, both short-term (e.g. storm events) and long-term (e.g. sea-level changes) (Cooper et al. 2018). Coastal barrier breaching is a common corollary of increased wave activity, extreme wind or storm events (Gómez et al. 2023). Barrier morphology and sediment unput whatsoever are of key importance in assessing their vulnerability to SLR (Houser et al. 2007; Maroukian et al. 2024). Sediment input decrease, particularly due to human interventions, has been shown to negatively affect coastal barriers and, thus, lagoons within relatively short time (Gatto and Carbognin 1981; Hzami et al. 2024).
Coastal lagoons require the supply of suspended solids from river networks to generate their sedimentary facies, which are subsequently deposited or resuspended within the water (Petti et al. 2018). Therefore, morphodynamic processes involving bottom geomorphology and water circulation lead to the distribution of surface sediment (Gardner and Doyle 2018). Due to the large contact surface area that is susceptible to resuspension and deposition, the interplay between aquatic and sedimentary processes is very dynamic (Gardner and Doyle 2018).
Remote sensing techniques are increasingly used in the study of coastal lagoons. They allow a precise monitoring of the lagoonal areas (including water quality, environmental challenges, land use patterns and sediment dynamics) (Ahmed et al. 2009; Kumar et al. 2016; Erena et al. 2019), repeatedly, over different time periods and at different scales (Huang et al. 2018). In this way, one can monitor the evolution of a lagoon and the fluctuations in its level (Apostolopoulos et al. 2022), but also its water quality (Erena et al. 2019). Geographic Information Systems (GIS) also offer a high potential for studying studying coastal lagoons. Mapping techniques have been employed to assess the suitability of coastal lagoons for specific activities, such as aquaculture. For instance, a study conducted on the southeast coast of India utilized Multi-Criteria Decision Support spatial analysis to map the aquaculture suitability of coastal lagoons based on multiple criteria (Jayanthi et al. 2021). Factors such as water quality, sediment composition, hydrological parameters, and ecological conditions were integrated to identify areas with high potential for aquaculture.
The physiographical features of water bodies, including coastal lagoons, are quantified through morphometry. Since morphometric measurements basically describe the topography and physical characteristics of coastal lagoons, they are rather simple to ascertain. Therefore, morphometry offers a straightforward way to characterize a system's inherent sensitivity, or susceptibility, to external loads and other artificial (mostly anthropogenic) alterations (Haines et al. 2006).
According to the IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (AR6), SSP1-2.6  projects a mean temperature rise of 1.6°C and a mean sea-level rise of 0.44 m (0.28–0.55 m). SSP5-8.5 projects a mean temperature rise of 4.3°C and a mean sea-level rise of 0.87 m (0.63–1.01) by 2100 at a global context, relative to the 1995–2014 baseline (Ali et al. 2022; IPCC 2023). These projections vary by region due to vertical land motion, ocean dynamics, and gravitational effects. For the Mediterranean region, global mean sea level is projected to rise by approximately 0.46 m (0.34–0.66 m) by 2100 under SSP1-2.6 and by 0.87 m (0.65–1.38m) under SSP5-8.5 (Ali et al. 2022; IPCC 2023).
In the present study, we aimed, initially, to map the coastal lagoons of Greece based on their physiographical characteristics and provide them through an interactive Web GIS application (accessible for free and without the creation of an account). Complementing this inventory, we investigated the recent temporal dynamics (2015–2025) of the five largest lagoon systems by analyzing decadal trends in their surface area and eutrophication levels using high-resolution Sentinel-2 satellite observations. Our further aim was to assess their vulnerability to future sea-level rise (SLR) by 2100, considering two different climatic scenarios, according to the IPCC (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change). By measuring key physiographical parameters of the lagoons (including the sandspit morphology), we examined which lagoons will be subject to partial or wholly inundation according to the two scenarios. For most lagoons we used revised values by Vecchio et al. (Vecchio et al. 2024), who have also incorporated VLM. In the other cases, we only considered the scenarios by IPCC (Ali et al. 2022), without considering VLM. Our analysis intentionally excludes complex factors like tectonics or future human activities to allow for broader application, rather than a case-specific approach. Future, more detailed assessment should consider other factors as well, including locally computed Vertical Land Motion (tectonism), sediment budget, lagoon bathymetry, more detailed morphological assessment, soil type and permeability etc.
Materials and methods
Coastal lagoon mapping and morphological analysis
For this paper, we mapped and digitalized all lagoons along the Greek coastline (also including the Greek islands) and measured their physiographical characteristics. Greece has one of the longest coastlines in Europe, spanning 13,676 kilometers in total. Greece's coastal zone is a rich natural resource, as it offers a wealth of coastal goods and services. Most Greeks live in coastal regions that are up to 1-2 kilometers from the coast. If we include those who live up to 50 kilometers from the shore in our definition of coastal population, then 85% of Greeks are coastal people. Twelve out of the thirteen regions of the Greek territory include coastal regions.
We used satellite images to map the lagoons. The Greek coastline was observed manually at scales ranging from 1:3,600 (for the smallest lagoons) to 1:36,000 for the largest lagoons (e.g. Vistonis). Google Earth was primarily selected, as it provides a wider temporal range for observations, also spanning up to recent years. Where no clear image could be obtained from Google Earth, the Hellenic Cadaster (Hellenic Cadastre 2021) was used. In each case, lagoons were digitized manually.
In order for an area to be mapped as a coastal lagoon, it was necessary for it to meet the following criteria: (a) be partly or fully filled with water in at least one satellite image (recent or older), or show signs of water activity, even if it is currently dry (Fig. 1a); (b) be partly or fully separated from the open sea via a sedimentary spit; (c) have an area of at least 300 m2; and (d) not be a part of a river bed (Fig. 1b). No differentiations were made regarding water salinity.

To investigate the factors influencing sandspit development in Greek coastal lagoons, we then selected the 10 largest lagoons across the country based on surface area. For each one of them, we examined the local geological regime of the broader area, in order to examine whether the lithological factor has played a role in the development and maintenance of a large sandpit. For this purpose, we utilized the geological sheets at 1:50,000 scale provided by the Hellenic Survey for Geology and Mineral Exploration (formerly IGME). We also examined whether the local wind and wave regime also bore an influence on the development of the sandspits. Data concerning wind direction and wave orientation were retrieved from the wind and wave atlas of Greece (Soukissian et al. 2007a).
Implications for the future sea-level rise
Subsequently we used the measurements of the sandspits in order to assess the effects of the projected mean sea-level rise (MSLR) by 2100. We considered one high emission (SSP5-8.5) and one low emission scenario (SSP1-2.6) of the IPCC, according to the IPCC AR6 WGII Cross-Chapter Paper 4 on the Mediterranean Region (Ali et al. 2022). For most lagoons, we used revised data from (Vecchio et al. 2024). As the area included in this work does not cover the whole coastline of Greece, their projections could not be used for some lagoons, in which case, the fixed IPCC projections (Ali et al. 2022) were used, predicting a MSLR of 0.46 m for SSP1-2.6 and 0.87 m for SSP5-8.5. The values used for each lagoon can be seen in Appendix A.
Vertical Land Motion (VLM) includes tectonic and, to a lesser extent, glacio-isostatic movements, which are particularly intense along the Mediterranean coastline (e.g., (Niki Evelpidou et al. 2023)). Future sea-level rise poses a significant threat to low-lying, subsiding coasts, necessitating assessment methods that account for both factors, as demonstrated in Southern Italy (Scardino et al. 2022). Therefore, VLM has been included in our assessment indirectly, by considering the revised projection of (Vecchio et al. 2024) where applicable. For the lagoons where this data did not exist, VLM has not been taken into consideration.
The results of our analysis and mapping have been compiled into an interactive Web GIS application, freely accessible at https://lagoons.vercel.app/ without requiring user registration. The application provides a comprehensive geospatial overview of all 152 mapped lagoons (Fig. 8). Each lagoon is represented by a color-coded point marker reflecting its projected inundation status under the two SLR scenarios: lagoons projected to be fully inundated under both SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 are displayed in red, those projected to be inundated only under SSP5-8.5 in orange, and those not projected to be fully inundated in teal (Fig. 8a). At higher zoom levels, the digitized lagoon boundary polygons become visible, allowing for a detailed spatial examination of each system's planform geometry. Users can select any lagoon to access a detailed attribute panel displaying all computed morphometric parameters (length, width, area, perimeter, sandspit dimensions), as well as the projected sea-level rise values for both the IPCC-only and the VLM-adjusted scenarios (Fig. 8b). The platform supports spatial queries through a multi-criteria query builder and offers filtering by geographic location, island, and inundation status, as well as free-text search by lagoon name (Fig. 8c). Multiple basemap layers, including satellite imagery, topographic and cartographic representations, are available to facilitate visual interpretation. On-map measurement tools for distance and area are also provided. This Web GIS serves as both a data dissemination platform and a decision-support resource for researchers and coastal zone managers.

Excluded parameters
Our results is a purely morphodynamics assessment of coastal vulnerability for the Greek lagoons and, as such, it is solely based on sandspit morphology and future SLR projections. This allows for a broader applicability of our model, allowing it to be implemented along the entire Greek coastline. Significant factors affecting coastal vulnerability of Mediterranean lagoons that have been excluded from our analysis include the following:
Natural evolution for coastal lagoons. Such environments will respond and change gradually to increasing sea level.
	Locally computed VLM, which are different and case-specific for each site.
	Rapid sea-level changes owed to a single seismic event. In Greece, rapid uplift or subsidence have been reported, owed to a single event (e.g. (Niki Evelpidou, Karkani, et al. 2021)). This can significantly affect coastal lagoons if present. Future predictions for such events have not been considered.
	Sediment budget. It refers to either positive or negative equilibrium between sediment input and removal and can significantly affect the sandspit stability (Zanchettin et al. 2007; Conde et al. 2019).
	Detailed sandspit morphology and lagoon bathymetry. A detailed topographic analysis of the lagoons’ bottom, as well as a more detailed sandspit morphology analysis using LiDAR or other techniques could provide more accurate results.
	Soil type and permeability. It can influence the hydraulic behavior of the lagoons relative to the sea (including saltwater intrusion and groundwater levels).
	Detailed analysis of meteorological and oceanographic factors, such as extreme sea-levels, storm surges, longshore currents, wave height and tidal range. This would provide a detailed analysis of coastal vulnerability.
	Human factors, including land use, infrastructure and demographics and existing (or planned) management practices.
We understand that the above methodology is an oversimplified approach to estimate coastal lagoon vulnerability, given that sand spit morphology and future sea-level rise projections are not the sole factors influencing coastal vulnerability. However, additional factors such as sedimentology and sediment balance, local morphodynamics (wave activity, long-shore currents, tidal range), morphology of the sea-bed and the mainland, potential fluvial input, as well as human activities could not be integrated for all Greek lagoons. Instead, they would have to be dealt with for each lagoon separately, which fell beyond the scope of this work.
Analysis of water surface extent and water quality
An analysis of water surface extent and water quality was conducted for the five largest Greek coastal lagoons: Vistonis, Kleisova, Rodia, Stroggyli and Tsoukali. Sentinel-2 MultiSpectral Instrument (MSI) Level-2A (atmospherically corrected surface reflectance) imagery was acquired through the Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem (CDSE) OData Application Programming Interface (API) (https://documentation.dataspace.copernicus.eu/APIs/OData.html; Accessed in December 15, 2025). For each lagoon, one cloud-free image was selected per season (winter: January, spring: April, summer: July, autumn: October) for each year, starting from the summer 2015 and until the winter of 2025, resulting in up to 42 observations per lagoon over the 11-year study period (with the exception of 1-2 missing observations due to the unavailability of cloud-free imagery in specific seasons). Seasonal composites (winter, spring, summer, autumn) were analyzed to capture intra-annual variability. The Sentinel-2 spectral bands utilized are shown in the following table (Table 2).
Table 2. Sentinel-2 MSI spectral bands used in this analysis.
Band	Description	Central Wavelength	Resolution
B03	Green	560 nm	10 m
B04	Red	665 nm	10 m
B05	Red Edge 1	705 nm	20 m
B08	NIR	842 nm	10 m
Water surface extent was mapped using the Normalized Difference Water Index (NDWI) (McFeeters 1996). The NDWI exploits the strong absorption of near-infrared radiation by water and its relatively high reflectance in the green band (equation 1):
NDWI = (Green − NIR) / (Green + NIR) (1)
Pixels with NDWI > 0 were classified as water (McFeeters 1996). The resulting binary mask required post-processing to address two categories of error: (1) commission errors from isolated bright pixels misclassified as water, and (2) omission errors from small gaps within the true water surface. Mathematical morphological operations were utilized following established protocols for remote sensing applications (Soille, 2004). First, a morphological opening was applied using a 3\times3 pixel matrix. This step was necessary to eliminate spatially isolated misclassified pixels caused by sensor noise or atmospheric artifacts, such as cloud shadows or mist, which otherwise result in false-positive water detection. Crucially, because the spatial footprint of the matrix (approximately 900m^2) is negligible compared to the extensive surface area of the lagoons, this operation effectively removes these artifacts without compromising the geometrical integrity or shrinking the boundaries of the primary water body. Subsequently, a morphological closing was performed to fill small data gaps caused by localized turbidity or floating vegetation, ensuring a continuous water mask. Finally, a connected component analysis was implemented to isolate the main lagoon body from surrounding unrelated water features. This algorithm groups individual water pixels into distinct objects based on 8-point connectivity, meaning pixels are treated as part of the same region if they touch along an edge or a corner. By calculating the area of each resulting object and retaining only the largest contiguous region, we effectively filtered out disconnected features, such as agricultural ponds, adjacent drainage canals, or ephemeral wetlands, that are detected by the satellite index but are not part of the lagoon system.
Water surface area (km²) was calculated by multiplying the water pixel count by the geographic pixel area, accounting for latitude-dependent variations in pixel size. Chlorophyll-a concentration, a key indicator of phytoplankton biomass and trophic status, was estimated using the Normalized Difference Chlorophyll Index (NDCI) (Mishra and Mishra 2012). The NDCI exploits the absorption of red light by chlorophyll and the reflectance peak in the red-edge region (equation 2):
NDCI = (Red Edge − Red) / (Red Edge + Red) (2)
Chlorophyll-a concentration (μg/L) was derived from NDCI using the empirical polynomial equation developed by Mishra & Mishra (2012) for turbid productive waters (R² = 0.92) (equation 3). Trophic state was classified according to the Carlson Trophic State Index thresholds (Carlson 1977) (Table 3).
Chl-a (μg/L) = 14.039 + 86.115 × NDCI + 194.325 × NDCI² (3)
Trophic state was classified according to the Carlson Trophic State Index thresholds (Carlson 1977). It is to be mentioned that the NDCI-to-Chl-a algorithm was originally calibrated for MERIS imagery; application to Sentinel-2 bands involves minor spectral differences, but it’s utilized by he community as a standard.

Table 3. Trophic state classification based on chlorophyll-a concentration (Carlson 1977).
Trophic state	Chlorophyll-a range	Ecological implication
Oligotrophic	< 2.6 μg/L	Low productivity, clear water
Mesotrophic	2.6 – 7.3 μg/L	Moderate productivity
Eutrophic	7.3 – 20 μg/L	High productivity, potential algal blooms
Hypereutrophic	> 20 μg/L	Very high productivity, anoxia risk

Long-term trends were assessed using Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) linear regression. The slope coefficient indicates the rate of change (km²/year for water extent; μg/L/year for chlorophyll-a), while the coefficient of determination (R²) and p-value indicate the strength and statistical significance of the trend. Trends were classified as:
Stable: |slope| < 0.1 km²/year (extent) or |slope| < 0.5 μg/L/year (Chl-a concentration)
Expanding/Shrinking: slope > 0.1 or < −0.1 km²/year
Worsening/Improving: slope > 0.5 or < −0.5 μg/L/year
Results
Mapping of the coastal lagoons
A total of 152 lagoons was identified along the Greek coastline. They are all included in Appendix A. The largest among them are found in the Amvrakikos Gulf, as well as in the area of Thrace. Large lagoons are also found in the Gulf of Patras. The highest accumulation of coastal lagoons, though small in size, is found in the islands of Naxos, Melos, Lesbos, Lemnos, as well as the coastline of Thesprotia, NW Greece , Argolis, Peloponnese and the Thermaic Gulf. In the latter case, however, only very small lagoons have been identified. Interestingly, the coasts of Crete, the Dodecanese, Euboea, the Corinthian and the Euboean Gulf only have a few coastal lagoons, mostly of very small sizes (less than 1.4 km2).
Six lagoons, representing 3.9% of the total, have considerably larger size than the rest of the lagoons. In particular, the lagoon known as Vistonis, which spans 42.5 km2, is situated in Thrace. The lagoons of Tsoukali and Rodia, which have respective areas of 30.1 and 20.0 km2, are located in the Amvrakikos Gulf. The Kleisova lagoon in Mesologgi, which has a total area of 22.4 km2, is ranked third. With a total area of 13 km2, the Stroggyli lagoon in the Amvrakikos Gulf is the fifth largest, also in the Amvrakikos Gulf.
3.9% of the lagoons have a length of < 100 meters. 17.7% of them have a length of 100-300 meters, 33.6% of them have between 300 and 1,000, and 18.4% of them have a length of 1,000-2,000 meters. The rest 26.3% have a length of more than 2 km. Concerning lagoon width, the <100 m category includes 14.5% of the total records, the 100-300 m 33.6%, the 300-1,000 m 28.3%, the 1,000-2,000 m 16.4% and the >2,000 m, 7.2%.
Nine of the 152 lagoons, representing 5.9%, have two sandspits separating them from the sea. These lagoons are as follows: Alykes Lefkimmis in Corfu, Aktion in Central Greece, Divari in the southwestern Peloponnese, Drepano in Thesprotia, Kalogera in northwestern Peloponnese, Asprolimni, in Lemnos, and a lesser lagoon near it, Aghios Andreas in Samothrace and Alyki in northeastern Peloponnese (for locations see the Web GIS).
We have plotted sandspit length and height per lagoon area (Fig. 3). The length of the sandspit does show an increasing trend per area – which is reasonable, even though a following pattern cannot be deduced. While most Greek lagoons show a length of <3,5 km², a surprisingly high clustering can be found in ~4 km² and ~5 to ~6.3 km², indicating that mesoscale lagoons are an important feature of the Greek coastline.
As regards sandspit height, it shows a nearly linear trend, with a few individual clusters of large-area lagoons. This indicates that, surprisingly, several large lagoons have a small sandspit and vice versa. The lagoons with the highest sandspits include Sani (26 m) and Glarokavos (12.8 m) in Chalcidice, Korissia (23.7 m) in Corfu, Aghia Eleni (16.3 m) in Lesbos island, Kaiafas  (11 m) in the Peloponnese and Nea Kessani (7.2 m) and Vistonis (6.8 m) in Thrace.

Connection with lithological and climatic factors
Considering the largest of the lagoons, lithological composition and wind–wave dynamics were found to significantly influence sediment transport and the formation of sandspits and dunes across Greek coastal lagoons. In several regions, Miocene marine sediments dominate the geological substrate, especially in the eastern sectors of alluvial deposits—such as in Thrace (Vistonis lagoon), the Amvrakikos Gulf (Tsoukali, Logarou, Rodia), and parts of western Greece including Mesologgi (Kleisova), Corfu (Korissia, Lefkimmi Saltpans), Thesprotia (Drepano), Lemnos (Asprolimni), and northeastern Peloponnese (Alyki) (e.g. (Maragkoudakis et al. 1970; Roussos et al. 1993)).
Marine sediments occur in the southwestern Peloponnese (Divari lagoon). Holocene - aged alluvial deposits, are found in a very small area. Limestones are the primary geological formations in northwestern Peloponnese, but there are also alluvial deposits to the east (Hellenic Survey for Geology and Mineral Exploration 2021). While there are deposits in the South, marine deposits predominate at Samothrace. Lastly, The Flamboura lagoon is the only lagoon in the Amvrakikos Gulf that is not entirely surrounded by alluvial deposits. Marine sediments may be found to the west of the lagoon (Hellenic Survey for Geology and Mineral Exploration 2021).
Alluvial and marine deposits, in combination with the winds that dominate the area, favor the creation of sand dunes. This is a result of the fine-grained sediments' wind-transportability. Lastly, limestone is the only lithology that is unfavorable for the transport of sediment. Nonetheless, the geology and predominant wind direction surrounding the individual lagoons seem to be crucial factors since they will determine the formation of sandspits.
Alluvial and marine deposits, particularly where fine-grained sediments are present, may favor aeolian transport and sand dune formation under prevailing wind conditions. In contrast, limestone-rich areas were less conducive to sediment transport. The prevailing wind and wave directions varied by region, generally correlating with sediment supply and transport dynamics: eastern winds in the Amvrakikos Gulf; bidirectional (NW–SE) in Corfu and Thesprotia; SE winds and NW gusts in southwestern Peloponnese (Divari); SW and occasional N winds on Lemnos; SW winds and NE waves in Samothrace; NW waves and S winds in Alyki; and W winds and waves in Vistonis (Soukissian et al. 2007b).
Notably, wind and wave activity was minimal in the northwestern Peloponnese and the Gulf of Patras (Kalogera and Kleisova lagoons). Among the examined sites, the presence and height of sand dunes were closely associated with reduced lagoonal exposure to marine inundation. For instance, the Korissia lagoon in Corfu is protected by a prominent sandspit reaching ~15 meters in height (Fischer et al. 2016). Overall, the combination of fine-grained sediment availability, dominant wind direction, and specific lithological contexts strongly governs the development of sandspits and dunes, which in turn affect lagoon stability and resilience to marine transgression.

According to our study, considering only the projections of IPCC (Ali et al. 2022) (i.e. excluding VLM), for the low emission scenario (SSP1-2.6), 12 out of 152 of the coastal lagoons of Greece (8%) will be fully inundated, and 22 (15%) will be inundated for the high emission scenario (SSP5-8.5) (Fig. 4a). If, however, VLM is also considered, by using the revised projections by Vecchio et al. (Vecchio et al. 2024), 18 lagoons will be fully inundated, representing 12%, for SSP1-2.6, and 25 lagoons (25%) for SSP5-8.5. Note that these numbers and percentages only refer to lagoons that will be fully inundated. The number of lagoons that will be partly breached is much higher. A prominent result of our analysis and literature review is that the highest sandspits bear sand dunes with significant development. Our findings also point that that well-developed sand dune systems play a critical role in protecting coastal lagoons from direct marine inundation. Foredune ridges, often exceeding 4 meters in elevation, function as natural barriers that can limit overwash events and saline intrusion, especially during high-energy storm surges and episodes of sea-level oscillation.
This finding is important, because sand dune restoration (where dunes are present) or artificial development (where they are absent) seems to be an effective and efficient means of protecting the coastal lagoons (see Discussion section). Discussion
The latest report from the IPCC (IPCC 2023) highlights the effects of climate change on estuaries and lagoons worldwide. Furthermore, the IPCC's forecasts for the future show a rise in the severity and frequency of severe events, which might lead to higher sea-levels. The rate of sea-level rise is expected to continue to rise in the coming decades, with potentially devastating consequences for coastal communities and ecosystems worldwide (Carrasco et al. 2016). The negative effects of flooding threats caused by sea-level extremes (ESLs) represent a danger to low-lying coastal communities and ecosystems globally (Nicholls et al. 2021). Projections indicate that future sea-level rise is the primary driver for the most extreme sea levels expected along global coastlines by 2100 (Jevrejeva et al. 2023).
Coastal lagoon management should consider the lagoons’ ecological and socioeconomic significance, as well as the hazards that threaten them. When assessing coastal flood vulnerability for management, both hydrodynamic and GIS modeling offer distinct advantages, and their suitability depends on the specific needs of the assessment (Seenath et al. 2016). Integrated coastal zone management (ICZM) is a strategy that attempts to maintain the biological integrity of coastal ecosystems while balancing the interests of different stakeholders (Haenssler et al. 2013). ICZM can focus on habitat preservation and restoration, human activity control, and the research and monitoring initiatives (Carrasco et al. 2016). Other strategies include applying sustainable fishing and aquaculture methods, as well as ecosystem-based management (EBM), which focuses on managing entire ecosystems as opposed to individual species (Chapman 2012; El Mahrad et al. 2022).
According to Adao et al. (Adao et al. 2022), monitoring of coastal lagoons is essential to better understand their ecological status and management. However, the monitoring of coastal lagoons can be challenging due to the complexity of the systems and the different factors that influence them. To improve the monitoring of coastal lagoons, Adao et al. (Adao et al. 2022) recommend using complementary sampling methods, including remote sensing, benthic sampling and hydroacoustic surveys. Through continued research and monitoring, sea-level change signals can be identified early enough to proceed to the necessary adaptations, which is a crucial condition for any adaptive decision-making method (Stephens et al. 2018).
4.1. Sea-level rise
Sea-level fluctuations are primarily driven by two factors: changes in the elevation of the land relative to the sea surface, and variations in the mass or volume of ocean water. Over the past 4,000 years, the contribution of ice-equivalent meltwater to sea-level rise has been negligible – typically less than 0.2 mm/year – compared to rates exceeding 3 mm/year over the last 100 years (Church et al. 2008). Therefore, most significant changes in relative sea level (RSL) during this period are attributed to vertical land movements caused by tectonic activity, glacial isostatic adjustment (GIA), and sediment compaction (Teatini et al. 2011).
Although sea-level rise is not uniform across the globe, regional variations of approximately ±30% around the global mean are expected. These differences are driven by factors such as thermal expansion, ocean circulation patterns, and the uneven distribution of land ice loss (IPCC 2023). In regions experiencing rapid vertical land movements, especially those caused by human activities such as groundwater extraction—deviations from the global average sea-level rise can exceed ±30% (IPCC 2023). The Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets have begun to lose mass faster during the past three decades, which has started to have a major impact on the current sea-level rise (Durand et al. 2022).
The ecological (natural system vulnerability) and socio-economic (socio-economic system vulnerability) aspects are the two components used to determine how vulnerable coastal resources are to the effects of sea-level rise. The rates at which the Mediterranean over the past two to three centuries has been estimated at 1.7-1.8 mm/a (Wöppelmann and Marcos 2012; Tsimplis et al. 2013). According to (Carillo et al. 2012), the Mediterranean Sea's unique features as a semi-closed basin that is only accessible to the Atlantic Ocean by the Strait of Gibraltar allow it to deviate from the patterns of sea-level rise observed worldwide.
Due to its relatively warm waters and regional oceanographic conditions, the Aegean Sea is considered one of the Mediterranean’s more vulnerable regions to sea-level rise, particularly from thermal expansion and associated regional effects (Wöppelmann and Marcos 2012). Naxos, for example, is a large island located in the Aegean Sea and more specifically in the Cyclades complex. Western Naxos is characterized by an abundance in sandy beaches, many of which are characterized by the presence of at least one coastal lagoon (Niki Evelpidou et al. 2012; Niki Evelpidou, Petropoulos, et al. 2021a; Niki Evelpidou et al. 2022). Melos island, Cyclades, is also characterized by a significant number of lagoons compared to its size (Pantazidou et al. 2006). The same is for Lemnos island (Triantaphyllou et al. 2023).
Sea-level rise brought about by climate change will put more coasts at danger. When combined with local erosion patterns and storm activity, this might result in extremely high flood risks. Due to the aforementioned, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC 2023) has classified the Mediterranean coastline as vulnerable owing to its high risk of flooding, coastal erosion and overall land degradation.
4.2. Spatial distribution and morphological diversity of the Greek lagoons
The coastal lagoons of Greece demonstrate significant geographic and morphological heterogeneity. The highest concentrations are in Naxos, Melos, Lesbos, Lemnos, Thesprotia and Argolis, while larger lagoons are concentrated in the Amvrakikos Gulf, Thrace and the Gulf of Patras. In contrast, islands like Crete and the Dodecanese host only few and small lagoons.
This spatial variation reflects the interplay between tectonic activity, sediment supply, and hydrodynamic energy, with low-energy coastlines and abundant alluvial deposits favoring lagoon formation. For example, Amvrakikos and Patras Gulfs, Thesprotia and Thrace host estuaries of significant rivers, including Louros and Arachtos; Thyamis; and Nestos, Evros and Lissos, respectively. These rivers transport significant amounts of sediments, favoring the development of large and/or multipart lagoonal systems.
The Amvrakikos Gulf coastline is surrounded by many lagoonal systems - including some of the largest in Greece. This is favored by two factors. On the one hand, Louros and Arachthos rivers contribute significantly to the configuration of the coastal zone, as they deposit high amounts of sediments along the coast (Kapsimalis et al. 2004; Vryniotis and Papadopoulou 2004). This is evident by the intense advancement of their deltas, as seen from satellite imagery. On the other hand, it is an enclosed Gulf, which favors the trapping of coastal sediments within its boundaries, preventing them from exiting the gulf.
Interestingly, the Thermaic Gulf, hosting the estuaries of four large rivers (Aliakmon, Loudias, Axios and Peneus), does not host any significant lagoonal systems in terms of size, even though several small lagoons are formed. This is arguably owed to the fact that wave activity in the Thermaic Gulf is relatively high, thus spreading sediments along the coast and not favoring the development of larger lagoons. At the same time, the coastline is open and straight in many parts, rather than inundated, which also does not favor lagoon development, contrary, e.g., to the Amvrakikos Gulf.
Despite the absence of significant rivers, some islands show surprisingly high clustering in coastal lagoons, either small or larger ones. These include Melos, Lemnos, Naxos, Antiparos islets and Lesbos. This is a combination of multiple factors, including: (a) Irregular coasts, forming several coves and outlets which favor the development of lagoonal systems. (b) Local tectonic subsidence, which is confirmed for the broader area of Naxos-Paros-Antiparos (N. Evelpidou et al. 2012; Niki Evelpidou et al. 2018) and Lemnos (Pavlopoulos et al. 2013). (c) Presence of coastal plains; while most islands in the Aegean are steep and rocky, these islands often have a more extensive flat coastal zone, favoring the development of lagoonal systems. (d) Potentially increased sediment supply, due to the presence of relatively non0resistant rocks, such as volcanics, granodiorite and marine deposits.
Geological and climatic controls on the Greek lagoons’ formation and stability
Our findings underscore the critical role of local lithology and wind–wave dynamics in shaping the geomorphological evolution and vulnerability of Mediterranean coastal lagoons – especially the larger ones. Miocene marine and Holocene alluvial deposits may provide the essential sediment sources required for sandspit and dune formation, particularly in regions with consistent aeolian energy. The widespread distribution of alluvial deposits around many lagoons supports their capacity to generate wind-blown sedimentary structures, while limestone-dominated substrates tend to inhibit such processes, due to their lower erodibility and coarser grain sizes. The spatial variability in wind and wave regimes further dictates the directionality of sediment transport and barrier development, making wind orientation a key driver in sandspit morphology.
It was observed that the winds and swell in the Amvrakikos Gulf, which is home to some of the largest Greek lagoons (Flamboura, Logarou, Tsoukali, and Rodia), are from the east. The winds blow both north-westerly and south-easterly in Corfu and Thesprotia (likes Lefkimmi, Korissia, and Drepano lagoons), while the waves are both northerly and south-easterly in direction. Additionally, the direction of the winds and waves in south-western Peloponnese, where Divari Lagoon is situated, is southeast, with wind gusts coming from the northwest. The winds and waves that blow into the lagoons of Asprolimni on the island of Lemnos originate from the south-west and occasionally from the north. The winds and waves at Samothrace are from the south-west and north-east, respectively, especially in the lagoon of Agios Andreas. The waves in the Alyki lagoon, in north-eastern Peloponnese, are northwesterly in direction and the winds blow from the south. Westerly winds and waves are present in Thrace's Vistonis lagoon. Last but not least, winds and waves are absent from the northwestern Peloponnese and the Mesological region, which are home to the lagoons of Kalogera and Kleisova, respectively (Soukissian et al. 2007b).
Importantly, the presence of elevated sand dunes along certain coastal fringes serves as a natural defense mechanism against marine inundation and erosion. Their geomorphological function has been documented not only under gradual sea-level rise scenarios but also during high-energy events such as storm surges and tsunamis (e.g., (Gogou et al. 2019)). Evidence has shown that the presence of sand dunes can protect the coast against both coastal erosion and inundation (Rhymes et al. 2015; Niki Evelpidou, Petropoulos, et al. 2021b; Niki Evelpidou, Petropoulos, et al. 2021a; Niki Evelpidou et al. 2022; Niki Evelpidou and Spyrou 2024a).
The Korissia sandspit in Corfu, for instance, which has the second largest largest sandspit height of ~24 m, provides a compelling example of dune-induced coastal resilience. Evidence supports the growing emphasis on nature-based solutions, such as dune stabilization and restoration, as cost-effective and sustainable methods for mitigating climate change-driven risks in coastal zones (Masria et al. 2015). In this context, understanding the interplay between lithology, sediment availability, and wind–wave dynamics is essential for lagoon conservation planning and hazard management.
Vulnerability of Greek lagoons to sea-level rise
Our analysis reveals a high level of vulnerability among Greek coastal lagoons. Considering the climatic scenarios only, and excluding VLM, under the SSP1-2.6 scenario, 8% of lagoons are projected to be inundated; under SSP5-8.5, the figure increases to 12%. When VLM is incorporated, the projections become more severe: 12 or 25% of lagoons will be inundated by 2100, according to optimistic and pessimistic emissions pathways.
This result aligns with a growing body of research highlighting the synergistic threat of eustatic sea-level rise and regional land subsidence in deltaic and coastal sedimentary environments (e.g., (Maroukian et al. 2024; Vecchio et al. 2024)). In particular, low-lying lagoons in western Greece, including the Amvrakikos Gulf and Mesologgi region (Gulf of Patras), and parts of northeastern Aegean (e.g., Vistonis lagoon) are at disproportionate risk, due to both elevation loss and high sediment compaction rates.
These findings underscore the urgent need for site-specific adaptation planning, including the protection of barrier systems, restoration of dune complexes, and monitoring of subsidence dynamics. Key adaptive measures should focus on:
	Stabilizing, preserving and reinforcing natural barrier systems, including sandspits, which currently protect many lagoons from direct marine inundation (e.g. (Eichmanns et al. 2021; Niki Evelpidou and Spyrou 2024b)).
	Restoring degraded dune complexes (e.g. (Johnston et al. 2023)), especially those that exhibit significant elevation (e.g., Korissia in Corfu, Chortatolimni in Lemnos), since these act as critical buffers against storm surges and rising seas (cf. (Gogou et al. 2019; Niki Evelpidou et al. 2022)).
	Implementing continuous subsidence monitoring, using satellite geodesy (e.g., InSAR, GNSS) and sediment compaction studies, to improve risk assessments and calibrate inundation models (Buzzanga et al. 2020).
Without these interventions, even lagoonal systems that seem relatively resilient today may lose their natural protection and become more exposed to climate change-driven challenges, including extreme weather events and sediment starvation, as well as human activities. Ultimately, adaptation to sea-level rise in Greek coastal lagoons must move beyond general shoreline protection to holistic, locally grounded and ecologically informed strategies, recognizing the critical role of geomorphic features and subsidence in shaping future coastal landscapes.
Water extent and quality implications
The multi-temporal analysis of water surface extent and water quality for the five largest Greek coastal lagoons reveals predominantly stable hydrological and trophic conditions, with notable exceptions that warrant attention. Among the five lagoons, Tsoukali demonstrated the only statistically significant change in water surface extent, classified as expanding with a positive trend of +0.192 km²/yr. This expansion may be linked to reduced sediment supply from upstream, as the construction of hydroelectric dams on Louros (1953) and Arachthos (1980) rivers has disrupted the natural sediment continuum, preventing suspended solids from reaching the gulf and lagoon system (Ramsar, 1998). The resulting sediment deficit may contribute to barrier erosion and lagoon expansion. Additionally, recent inundation modelling has identified the northern Amvrakikos coastline as highly prone to flooding under projected sea-level rise scenarios (Rossi et al., 2025), suggesting that the observed expansion may represent an early manifestation of climate change impacts on this deltaic system.
All five lagoons maintained eutrophic conditions throughout the monitoring period (mean Chl-a: 13.6–17.0 µg/L), consistent with the baseline trophic state typically observed in Mediterranean coastal lagoons subject to agricultural runoff, aquaculture, and urban nutrient loading (Pérez-Ruzafa et al., 2011). Vistonis lagoon was recorded as the most ecologically stressed system with the highest mean chlorophyll-a concentration (17.0 ± 4.8 µg/L), the greatest variability (maximum 38.8 µg/L, reaching hypereutrophic levels), and seven anoxia events. This elevated stress is attributable to its connection with the Porto Lagos–Lafri lagoon complex, which has been documented as hyper-eutrophicated, with nuisance cyanobacterial blooms dominated by Limnoraphis (Lyngbya) species that cause oxygen depletion during biomass decomposition and sedimentation (Orfanidis et al., 2019).
In contrast, the Amvrakikos Gulf lagoons (Rodia, Stroggyli, Tsoukali) recorded zero anoxia events despite documented pollution pressures from agricultural runoff, livestock farming, and historically inadequate wastewater treatment infrastructure (Ramsar, 1998). This resilience is attributable to their shallow depth (<2 m; Ramsar, 1998), which prevents the thermal stratification necessary for bottom-water oxygen depletion—a protective mechanism confirmed in comparative studies of semi-enclosed coastal water bodies experiencing hypoxia (Deltares, 2025).
Kleisova lagoon, part of the Mesologgi-Aitoliko complex—the largest lagoon system in Greece—similarly exhibited stable conditions with no anoxia events. However, the adjacent Aitoliko Lagoon is characterized by persistent stratification and permanent bottom anoxia, with high hydrogen sulfide concentrations and documented fish kills linked to storm-driven mixing of anoxic bottom waters (Koutsodendris et al., 2015; Deltares, 2025). The isolation of Kleisova from these conditions reflects its shallower, better-mixed hydrodynamics.
The above observations indicate that shallow depth provides inherent resilience against stratification-induced anoxia in Mediterranean coastal lagoons, though it does not protect against chronic eutrophication. The Tsoukali expansion trend warrants continued monitoring as a potential early-warning indicator of barrier instability and sea-level rise impacts, particularly given the documented reduction in sediment supply from upstream dam construction. Vistonis requires priority management attention to address nutrient loading and protect endemic fish populations from recurring hypoxic stress.
Conclusions
In this work, we have mapped all coastal lagoons along the Greek shoreline, based on satellite images and GIS analysis. We then computed key geomorphological features, including lagoon length and width, sandspit length, width and height. Based on those characteristics, we estimated the vulnerability of Greek lagoons to sea-level rise by 2100, by considering the future projections of the IPCC. We considered two scenarios: the optimistic one (SSP1-2.6 MSLR of 0.46 m) and the pessimistic one (SSP5-8.5 –MSLR of 0.87 m). We have also considered revised projections for MSLR, which include Vertical Land Motion (VLM) as well. All the above have been compiled into an interactive Web GIS application, publicly available at https://lagoons.vercel.app/.
A total of 152 lagoons were identified, ranging from very small ones (in the order of 10⁻³ km²) to larger ones (more than 20 km²). Most coastal lagoons are separated from the sea via a sandspit, but there were a few cases where two sandspits had been developed. The lagoons of Greece show significant spatial and morphological heterogeneity. The biggest clustering of small lagoonal systems were identified in Naxos, Melos, Lesbos, Lemnos islands, Thesprotia, Argolis and the Thermaic Gulf. Their presence is associated with the irregular coasts often found in these and most Greek islands, allowing for the development of coves and outlets and thus the formation of lagoons, local tectonic subsidence which favors lagoon development, the presence of wide coastal plains and low-lying coastal zones (which contradicts the landscape of most Aegean islands) and the potentially increased sediment input, due to the presence of non-resistant rocks.
Some of the largest Greek lagoons were found in the Amvrakikos Gulf, Thrace and the Gulf of Patras. They are associated with increased sediment supply from large rivers, including Louros, Arachtos, Thyamis, Nestos, Evros and Lissos. Out of these, Amvrakikos Gulf hosts a significant clustering of very large lagoons, which is owed to a combination of high sediment yield of the rivers and its nearly enclosed structure, which prevents coastal sediments from being removed.
An interesting finding of our study is that, despite the presence of four major river estuaries, the Thermaic Gulf bears significant clustering of lagoons, but very small in surface area. This is due to the high wave activity within the Gulf, as well as to the presence of large, nearly straight coastal segments.
The formation, morphology and geomorphological evolution of the Greek coastal lagoons is highly affected by the local lithological regime, at least as far as the largest ones are considered. In most of them, the presence of either alluvial depiosits or loosely consolidated marine deposits is significant, feeding the coastal system with sediments required to form sandspits.
Sea-level rise seems to be a factor with great impacts on the Greek lagoons, with 8 to 25% of them facing severe threat of complete inundation by 2100, according to the future climatic projections considered, while the proportion of the ones that will be partly breached is expected to be much higher.
Furthermore, a prominent geomorphological feature that was identified includes the development of extensive, often highly elevated sand dune systems atop the sandspits. The most typical example is Sani lagoon in Chalcidice and Korissia lagoon in Corfu, whose sandspits are the highest among the Greek lagoons (24-26 m). The presence of sand dune systems plays a key role in shielding coastal lagoons from sea-level inundation and coastal erosion. This points one the one hand to the urgent need for protecting the Greek lagoons against future SLR, and in the other hand, to the importance of protecting, restoring and./or artificially creating well-developed sand dune systems as a cost-effective and efficient method to protect the Greek lagoons.
The decadal satellite monitoring (2015–2025) of the five largest lagoon systems confirmed overall hydrological stability, though Tsoukali lagoon stands out as an exception, its progressive expansion signals the downstream consequences of dam-induced sediment starvation on barrier integrity. Water quality assessments placed all five systems within the eutrophic range, yet their ecological responses diverge markedly. Vistonis emerges as the most vulnerable, with repeated hypoxic episodes tied to external nutrient inputs, while the shallower Amvrakikos and Kleisova lagoons appear buffered against oxygen depletion by their limited depth. Collectively, these observations reinforce the need for management approaches that address site-specific stressors, particularly upstream sediment interception and catchment nutrient loads, in parallel with broader climate adaptation measures
